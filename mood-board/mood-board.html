Competitive Reference
What comparable programs do — what to steal, what to avoid
Substack
"Make money doing the work you believe in"
Strongest structural reference. White background, charcoal text, purple accent. Leads with creator empowerment + anti-establishment framing. Three stats front and center. "90% goes to you" as standalone callout — not buried.
Pattern: creator empowerment → validate with numbers → close with financial specifics
Steal: stat block placement Steal: anti-establishment copy Steal: 90%/advance as standalone
CauseChampions
"Post with a Purpose"
Closest direct analog. Dark navy, mission-forward tone. "Use your voice. Amplify what matters." Leads entirely with autonomy + authenticity — never opens with payment. Creator testimonials with follower counts. Accordion FAQ at bottom.
Differentiator: "Get paid or go pro bono — your call." Signals respect for the creator's agency.
Steal: lead with voice/cause Steal: dark navy tone Steal: testimonials w/ follower counts
Pinterest Inclusion Fund
Cause-aligned outcomes + clean data
Best outcome-led proof. Leads with creator results: "250% increase in saves," "147x outbound clicks." Minimal white layout. Eligibility checklist is fully transparent — no mystery about who qualifies. Mission-aligned but never preachy.
Uses outcome stats instead of platform stats — makes the creator feel like they gain something, not that they join something.
Steal: outcome stats not platform stats Steal: eligibility transparency
SNIF / Brand Ambassador
6-section recruitment page structure
Best page structure reference. Hero → 3-step visual → ideal partner traits → "The Assignment" → journey arc → perks + CTA. Uses "apply" not "join" to signal selectivity. Treats recruitment like a product launch.
Key insight: the "journey from creator to collaborator" arc maps perfectly onto Change Ambassadors (you care → you build → you get paid + people sign).
Steal: page flow Steal: "apply" selectivity signal Steal: journey arc narrative
Gymshark Athletes
"Keep focusing on your own path"
Best identity-before-compensation frame. Leads with values/mission not money. "Identify what makes you unique." Compensation mentioned late. Recruits on creator identity, not deal size.
Applicable pattern: "Your voice, your cause, your audience" — before any mention of payment structure.
Steal: identity-first framing Avoid: burying payment entirely (we should name the advance)
Change.org Help Center
Current baseline — what we're replacing
The gap we're closing. Documentation, not recruitment. Sidebar nav, bullet lists, no visual identity. Leads with compensation then jumps to disclosure policy. No social proof, no testimonials, no stats, no emotional hook.
Every design decision on the new page should be the opposite of this page.
Avoid: help center layout Avoid: disclosure above fold Avoid: no visual identity

Copy Patterns
Headlines and phrases worth stealing / adapting
Substack — adapt for Change Ambassadors
"Make money doing the work you believe in."
Lead with belief, close with money. This order matters — it signals the program respects the creator's motivation before pitching the deal.
CauseChampions — closest analog
"Post with a Purpose."
Use your voice. Amplify what matters.
Three words collapse two motivations (self-expression + impact). "Start something real" or "Turn your platform into a petition" does the same work for us.
Kelsey's actual outreach emails — use verbatim
"Guaranteed payment to start and share a petition that reflects your authentic voice."

"Full creative control, and if it gains momentum there's a chance to earn more."
These phrases are already converting in email. The landing page should mirror them — not rewrite them. Continuity between email and landing page reduces cognitive load.
Book publishing analogy — advance + royalties
"Your guaranteed advance."
"Advance" carries more dignity than "payment" or "compensation." Publishers have used advance-against-royalties for 100+ years — creators who write/advocate recognize this frame immediately.
SNIF — selectivity signal
"Apply" not "Join"
One word signals that we're selective, which makes acceptance feel earned. "Join the program" feels like a form. "Apply to partner with Change.org" feels like an opportunity.
Ad Council — messenger archetype frame
"The messenger is as important as the message."
Change Ambassadors can use this to frame WHY influencers (not just Change.org staff) matter — the credibility they've built with their audience is the thing we're partnering with.
Recommended Page Layout
Section order — synthesized from 6 reference programs
1 — Hero
Bold headline + 1-sentence subhead + single yellow CTA button + ghost "Book a call" link
No form above fold. One CTA only. Emotional hook first.
2 — Kelsey Video Card
3-min overview video + 4-question context grid (What is it / Who's it for / What do I do / What do I get)
Humanizes the program immediately after hero. Reduces "is this legit?" anxiety.
3 — Stat Block
3 horizontal numbers: petitions created · total signatures · average advance range
Trust accelerator. All 6 reference programs use this pattern. Numbers = credibility.
4 — Deliverables Grid
What you do (petition + shares) vs. what you get (advance + promo revenue). Advance shown as tier range — never a specific amount.
Advance-agnostic constraint. Show range, never promise a number.
5 — "This isn't a brand deal"
4-up differentiator grid: Creative control · Authentic topics · Guaranteed advance · Long-term relationship
Most important differentiator from standard brand deals. Lead with control, not money.
6 — Earnings Calculator
Promo revenue only (no advance shown). Slider: follower count → estimated promo revenue range.
Advance-agnostic constraint applies here too. Calculator = engagement + time-on-page.
7 — Tier Preview Chart
Show tier structure and advance ranges. Not "your advance" — "what tier you might be in."
Transparency builds trust. Don't hide the structure — just don't personalize it before contract.
8 — Proof Points / Recent Wins
Real starter examples: name, niche, petition topic, signatures. No fake testimonials.
Social proof with follower counts (per CauseChampions pattern). Specificity = credibility.
9 — How It Works (3 Steps)
Sign contract → Create petition → Share with your audience
Appears in every reference program. Reduces friction by making the ask feel small.
10 — Footer CTA
Repeat the yellow CTA button + "Book a call with Kelsey" ghost link
Close with same energy as the hero. Bookend structure.

Design Rules
Hard calls from the research
Do
  • Lead with the creator's voice and cause — not the contract
  • Use "guaranteed advance" language — it carries dignity (publishing frame)
  • Show the advance as a tier range, never a specific amount
  • Use Commissioner bold at 52px+ for the H1
  • One CTA above the fold — yellow button only
  • Put the stat block in the first scroll — 3 numbers, horizontal
  • Include real starter names + niches in proof points (specificity = trust)
  • Mirror Kelsey's actual outreach copy — don't rewrite what's already converting
  • Use "apply" language somewhere — signals selectivity
  • Name the "creative control" benefit explicitly — it's a conversion driver
Don't
  • Show a specific advance amount — it's advance-agnostic until contract negotiation
  • Put tier tables or fine print above the fold
  • Use two competing CTAs above the fold
  • Open with compensation — lead with cause/voice first
  • Use stock photos of generic influencers
  • Add the earnings calculator to the advance (promo revenue only)
  • Write disclosure/compliance language in the hero section
  • Use "join" — it's passive. Use "apply" or "partner"
  • Make it look like the Change.org help center — that's documentation, not recruitment
  • Use more than one font family — Commissioner + Inter only
